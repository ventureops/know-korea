## Our Story
I've spent a lot of time on planes.
Over 20 years working in large Korean corporations meant frequent business trips — across Asia, Europe, the Americas. New cities, new faces, new meeting rooms. But somewhere between the airport and the hotel, I always had the same feeling.
_I wish I actually understood this place._
Not just where to eat or what to see. I wanted to know **why** people here behave the way they do. What shaped their politics. How their history made them who they are today. What it actually feels like to live and work here — not as a tourist passing through, but as a real person trying to connect.
I'd search online. I'd find travel blogs, Wikipedia articles, the occasional expat forum thread from 2014. Nothing that gave me the full picture. Nothing that felt honest, current, and genuinely useful.
That feeling stayed with me.
And then I started thinking — Korea is the country I know best. I've worked inside its biggest companies. I've navigated its systems, its culture, its unwritten rules. I've watched foreigners arrive full of excitement, then spend months confused and frustrated by things that nobody had clearly explained.
**So I built the place I always wished existed.**
Not just a how-to guide. A real introduction to Korea — the practical stuff, the cultural stuff, the historical stuff, and everything in between. Written for the person who wants to understand, not just survive.

## How This Works
**Everything is free.** No paywall. No email gate. No premium tier. Every guide is open to anyone who needs it.
**The community makes it better.** Have a question that isn't answered here? Post it in Q&A. Other members answer — and the best questions shape future guides.
**We're honest about what we don't know.** When something requires a lawyer, a tax accountant, or an immigration specialist, we'll say so clearly. This site is a starting point, not a substitute for professional advice.

## A Note on Why This Matters
When you're in a foreign country — whether for a week, a year, or a decade — there's a difference between being _present_ and truly _understanding_.
Understanding why a colleague reacted a certain way in a meeting. Knowing the history behind a neighborhood you're walking through. Recognizing what a political headline actually means for daily life.
That kind of knowledge doesn't just make you more effective. It makes the experience richer. It changes how you see people. It turns a foreign place into somewhere that starts to feel like home.
That's what Know Korea is for.
